# Challenge a talk: depth, audience, relevance

The companion to [SKILL.md](./SKILL.md). That file is how to run the challenge and call the verdict; this is the sharp questions under each axis — what real depth looks like, whose problem the talk actually solves, and who is in the room. A weak idea caught here costs an afternoon; caught from the stage it costs the room's trust.

Hold this bar for the speaker's own idea, not just someone else's. The hardest idea to challenge honestly is the one you've already decided to give.

## 0. Steelman first

Before any objection, write the **strongest** case for giving this talk — the version a fan would make, not the one easiest to knock down. State the idea, the angle, and the promise to the listener in two or three lines. If you can't, the idea isn't formed yet — that's the first finding, and it's too early to challenge the rest.

## 1. Depth — is there a *there* there?

The question behind every "deep enough?": **could a listener get this faster without you?** If the same forty minutes spent reading the docs would land them in the same place, the stage adds nothing.

A rough ladder — name where the idea sits:

- **L0 — restatement.** The docs, the README, the changelog, read aloud. A listener could google it in the time you'd be talking.
- **L1 — tidy compilation.** Known material, well organized. Useful to a beginner; thin for a conference slot.
- **L2 — earned experience.** "Here's what bit us in production, and what we'd do differently." Non-obvious because you paid for it.
- **L3 — a new lens.** A model or angle that changes how the listener thinks about the topic afterward, not just what they know.

L0–L1 ideas almost always need a reframe to reach L2+, or a drop. The sharp tests:

- **Could they google it?** If yes, in the talk's own time, cut or go deeper.
- **So what?** Ask it of the core claim three times running. If the answers run out, the depth does too.
- **What's the twist?** Name the part that's non-obvious, counter-intuitive, or genuinely contested. No twist, no reason to gather a room.
- **The retelling test.** Two questions, and the talk needs both: *what should the listener carry out?* and *what will they tell a colleague about it afterward?* Write the one sentence they'd repeat in their own words at the coffee machine. If you can't, there's no core — only material, and an idea that doesn't survive retelling doesn't leave the room.

**Anti-patterns:** "everything about X in 40 minutes" (a survey with no angle); ten disconnected tips with no spine; "intro to X" with no pain driving it; a tour of a release's feature list.

## 2. Relevance — does this audience need it, now?

Depth without relevance is a good talk for the wrong year, or the wrong room. Interesting-to-the-speaker is not the same as needed-by-the-audience.

- **Whose problem is it?** The room's, or the speaker's pet interest? Watch the *false consensus* — "it fascinated me, so it'll fascinate everyone." Point to where this audience actually hits this.
- **Frequency × pain.** A frequent small ache or a rare large one earns attention; a rare small one earns none. Which is it, honestly?
- **Timing.** Not so late that the room has already moved on or migrated; not so early that no one is on this stack yet. Both fail the same way — empty nods.
- **The Monday test.** What does a listener do differently on Monday? If the honest answer is "nothing, but it was interesting," that's infotainment — fine, but name it, and don't sell it as practical.
- **The competition.** Has this been given at every conference for two years? Then relevance rides entirely on the twist from axis 1 — say what makes this version worth the slot over the others.

## 3. The audience — who is actually in the room?

The axis speakers skip. "Developers" is not an audience.

- **"For everyone" is for no one.** A talk pitched at juniors and principals at once usually loses both. Pick the seat you're aimed at.
- **Level calibration.** Over their heads and they lose the thread; under their feet and they're bored. What does this room *already* know — the base you will not re-explain (this is what a `SPEAKER-PROFILE.md` pins down)? Pitch one notch above it.
- **The right room?** Is this the venue and track where these people are — or a backend deep-dive booked onto a frontend day? A strong talk in the wrong room still fails.
- **Who is this NOT for?** If you can't name who should skip it and why, the audience is a blur and so is the talk. A clear "not for you if…" is a sign the target is real.
- **Size and format.** A narrow, deep niche plays to 20 in a workshop and dies in a 500-seat keynote slot — and the reverse. Match the depth to the room you're actually given.

## 4. Speaker self-deceptions

The biases that stop a speaker from challenging their own idea — name them out loud when they show:

- **Sunk cost** — "I already did the research, so I should give the talk." The research being done is not a reason the room needs it.
- **Novelty / hype** — new is not the same as needed. The audience doesn't owe attention to a thing for being recent.
- **Curse of knowledge** — "this is obvious" (to you, after months in it; not to the room) and its twin, "everyone finds this as interesting as I do."
- **Confirmation** — hunting for why the idea is good instead of where it breaks. Spend the first pass looking for the breaks.

## The verdict

End every challenge on exactly one. The verdict is the help; the questions were just how you got there.

- **Go** — holds depth, relevance, and a clearly named audience. Pin the angle and the one takeaway, and move to the outline.
- **Reframe** — the topic is alive but the angle, the promise, or the title misses the depth or the room. The most common outcome, and the most useful. Hand back the sharper angle, not just "this doesn't work."
- **Narrow** — real, but too much for the slot or aimed at too wide a room. Cut to one axis and one level; the rest becomes a future talk. Don't compress three talks into one — split.
- **Drop** — no depth to reach, or no real pain for any audience you can actually book, and no reframe rescues it. The cheapest good outcome: it frees the slot, and the speaker, for an idea that holds.

## The checklist

Run it on the idea, strongest doubt first:

1. Steelman written — can you state the best case for the talk?
2. Depth named on the ladder (L0–L3); the retelling test passes — one sentence the listener repeats to a colleague.
3. Could-they-google-it answered, and there's a twist.
4. Whose pain it solves is the room's, not the speaker's; the timing holds.
5. Monday test passes, or the talk is honestly labeled as not-practical.
6. One audience and one level, calibrated to what the room already knows.
7. You can name who this is *not* for.
8. A verdict is called — go / reframe / narrow / drop — with a reason and a next step.
