# AI tells: what gives machine-written prose away

A checklist for the read-aloud pass. **These are yellow flags, not bans.** Each hit is a reason to re-read the line and ask "would I say this out loud?" — not a reason to auto-replace. The goal is to kill the machine rhythm, not to sterilize the voice. Read "Don't touch" at the end first.

The strongest tell is not any single word — it's **rhythm and symmetry**. Hunt structure first, lexicon second.

## 1. Skeleton constructions (the big one)

What makes prose feel "assembled from the right words."

- **"not X, but Y" / "it's not just X — it's Y"** — balanced antithesis. Once is a device; three to six times in a row is a machine skeleton. Norm: 1, maybe 2 per short piece; in long form, proportionally more, but never stacked.
- **Anaphora chains:** "somewhere…, somewhere…, somewhere…", "maybe…, maybe…", three or more identical openers in a row.
- **Symmetric section grids:** "Two axes" → "Two mistakes" → "Three places" back to back, each with exactly three identical bullets. Real writing is lumpier — two points here, five there, a paragraph instead of a list somewhere else.
- **Triads on autopilot:** "speed, cost, and stability", "prompts, tools, context" — a by-three list in every paragraph. One or two per piece is fine; past that it's a tic.
- **"X is about Y"** as a stamp, if it shows up more than twice.

## 2. Unsupported generalizations and appeals to the crowd

Readers carry conclusions into their own work — "everyone / most / often / it's accepted" with no support is either taste or pressure-by-headcount. Both erode trust.

- "like everyone", "as most do", "as is customary"
- "many do this", "everybody loves / nobody likes"
- "it's a very common problem", "everyone's run into"
- "as is well known", "it's no secret that", "common knowledge"
- "it's generally held that" — without saying by whom
- "works everywhere / in all versions / always / in any project" — especially as the residue of compression that dropped the *why*

Fix: first-person experience ("I was looking for…", not "everyone looks for…"); a generalization only with support — an observation, a number, a link, a reproducible example; no support → cut it or mark it as a hypothesis / personal opinion.

## 3. Filler connectives (cut almost always)

- "It's worth noting that", "It's important to understand that", "Here it's worth saying"
- "Let's figure out / imagine", "You'll agree that"
- "And here's where it gets interesting", "and this is exactly where"
- "As practice shows", "it's no secret that"
- "But first things first"

## 4. Empty intensifiers

- "truly", "really", "genuinely", "the very"
- "a whole range / a whole set of", "a huge number of"
- "nothing other than"
- "key", "fundamental", "critically important"

## 5. Bureaucratese and nominalizations

- "represents", "serves as an example/solution", "plays an important role"
- "performs the management of", "enables the execution of", "carries out the processing"

## 6. Stock metaphors

- "in the world of development", "in today's world", "at the intersection of"
- "powerful tool", "flexibility and scalability", "seamless"
- "there's no silver bullet", "not a panacea", "a double-edged sword"
- poeticizing for its own sake: "the boundaries blur with every release"

## 7. Summary endings

- "Ultimately it all comes down to…", "And that changes everything"
- "That's the essence of it", "The main thing to remember is…"

## Don't touch (so you don't sterilize)

Without this block the list flattens the prose — the exact mistake it's meant to catch.

- honest author hedges: "in my experience", "in the typical case", "worth re-checking here" — that's voice, not filler;
- a one-off "a plus here, a minus there" hedge — fine in moderation;
- apt jargon ("under the hood", "out of the box") when it's doing real work;
- live "was X → became Y" images at the head of a block — they carry the block's weight; cut them last.
